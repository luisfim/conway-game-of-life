The History of Conway's Game of Life

Conway's Game of Life is a cellular automaton created by British mathematician John Horton Conway in 1970. It is often described as a zero-player game: after the initial pattern is created, the system evolves by itself.

The game became famous after Martin Gardner wrote about it in the October 1970 issue of Scientific American. Its popularity came from a strange contrast: the rules are extremely simple, but the patterns can become surprisingly complex.

The Rules

Underpopulation

A live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies.

Survival

A live cell with two or three live neighbors survives.

Overpopulation

A live cell with more than three live neighbors dies.

Birth

A dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes alive.

Classic Patterns

Blinker

A simple oscillator. It alternates between two states forever.

Glider

A moving pattern that travels diagonally across the grid.

Pulsar

A larger oscillator and one of the most recognizable Life patterns.

Why It Matters

Game of Life is important because it shows how complex behavior can emerge from simple rules. It is used as a classic example of cellular automata, simulation, emergence, and computational thinking.
